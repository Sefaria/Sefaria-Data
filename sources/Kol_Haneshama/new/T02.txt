/SCC/XXOMMENTARY/XX. The five psalms of /ITKabbalat Shabbat/XX, Psalms 95-99, are a
literary unit within the Book of Psalms. Their spirit of joy and exultation
calls on the worshipper to rejoice in the presence of God that fills the
world. Nature itself joins in the symphony of praise as heaven and earth,
field and forest, sea and rivers all seem to clap hands and enter the song
and dance of joy. The coming of Shabbat fills us with a new vision, one
that sees earth as freshly created and brimming with divine radiance. This
vision is a universal one. It invites all nations to join in our celebration of
divine presence.    /SC/XXA.G./XX/PR

/SCC/XXOMMENTARY/XX. It may be the concluding line of Psalm 95 that caused this
entire group of psalms to be included in /ITKabbalat Shabbat/XX. The psalmist
speaks of the forty years in the wilderness, saying that the generation who
came out of Egypt were not able "to enter Godòs place of rest," the Holy
Land promised at the end of Israelòs wanderings. But here in /ITKabbalat
Shabbat/XX "rest" has taken on a new meaning; it is in /ITtime/XX rather than in /ITplace/XX
that rest is to be found. Shabbat has herself become a Holy Land, a time
of rest in which we are called upon to cease our wandering.    /SC/XXA.G./XX/PR

/SCK/XXAVANAH/XX. The belief in the sovereignty of God should keep in our minds
the prophetic teaching that God should be obeyed rather than worshipped,
that obedience to Godòs laws is the highest form of worship. It is an error
to believe that the main function of the spiritual is to afford us an escape
from the turmoil and the temptations of life--a sort of ivory tower of
detachment. The truth of the sovereignty of God should remind us that
our task is to turn temptations into a means of serving God./PR

/SCC/XXOMMENTARY/XX. Psalm 29, one of the most ancient in the Book of Psalms,
celebrates the presence of God in the midst of a great thunderstorm. The
word /ITkol/XX, which appears seven times in the psalm, here translated "voice,"
can also mean "thunderclap." The psalmist concludes with mention of a
great quiet that follows the storm, recalling the mythic quiet that followed
Godòs triumph over the forces of chaos in Creation. The placing of the
psalm here reminds us that our weekly struggle in the world of achieve-
ment and bustle is now at an end. We have repeated the struggles of cre-
ation and now we too are called upon to achieve that great inner quiet
which is the secret of true rest.    /SC/XXA.G./XX/PR
