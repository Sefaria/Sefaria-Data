Kedushat Hayom<small> in Shabbat Shaḥarit</small> traditionally beginswith <i>Yismaḥ Moshe</i>, a paragraph describing Moses with head a glow bring-ing the Ten Commandments down from Mt. Sinai. It is omitted herebecause the sharpness of the imagery suggests a literal belief in the Sinaievent rather than an affirmation of its mythic truth. In its place"<i>Ashreynu</i> / Happy are we" evokes the special nature of the joyous Jewishheritage of which Shabbat is so integral a part.    D.A.T.K<small>AVANAH</small>. Consider your own creative power. Think about the work youhave done in the week that has passed, and feel your own pride and pleas-ure in that work. Then, try to set your work aside. Try not to think aboutthe work ahead next week. Rather, take a few long, deep breaths, slowdown, and enjoy the chance for rest that Shabbat brings. The concept of covenant is the central,organizing relationship which binds the Jewish people to the Jewish faithand Jewish fate. The idea of covenant connotes that steadfast devotion(<i>ḥesed</i>) to all that God would want for us and demand of us. Shabbat is aneternal sign of this eternal covenantal relationship between us andGod. In addi-tion to "remembering" (Exodus 20:8) and "keeping" (Deuteronomy 5:12)the Shabbat, we are also told to "honor and rejoice" in it (Isaiah 58:13).It would be well if our observance of Shabbat led us to the experience ofhonor and delight. While the rabbis spoke of <i>oneg shabbat</i>, Sabbath delight,as referring to bodily pleasures such as eating well, Isaiah spoke of onegshabbat in connection with his vision for a socially just world. There arespiritual as well as physical delights of Shabbat to be nurtured andenjoyed.
